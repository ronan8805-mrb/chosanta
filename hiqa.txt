Secure Living Support Services Ltd
End-of-Life Care Policy

Policy No.: SLSS-P015
Effective Date: [Insert Date]
Review Date: [Insert Date – typically 12 months later]

1. Purpose
The purpose of this policy is to ensure that every resident of Secure Living Support Services Ltd receives compassionate, dignified, and person-centred care as they approach the end of life. Our commitment is to:
- Promote comfort, respect, and quality of life in the final stages of life.
- Honour the cultural, spiritual, and personal values and wishes of each resident.
- Support residents, their families, and significant others with sensitivity and empathy.
- Provide a structured framework for staff to deliver consistent and high-quality end-of-life care.
- Ensure compliance with national standards, ethical principles, and relevant legislation, including the Health Act 2007 and HIQA’s National Standards for Residential Services for Children and Adults with Disabilities.
2. Scope
This policy applies to all staff, healthcare professionals, volunteers, and associated care partners involved in the care of residents approaching the end of life within Secure Living Support Services Ltd.
3. Principles
- Respect for the dignity, rights, and wishes of each resident.
- Holistic and individualised care planning.
- Effective communication and collaboration with residents, families, and healthcare teams.
- Provision of physical, emotional, psychological, spiritual, and social support.
- Advance care planning and early identification of end-of-life needs.
- Compliance with legal and ethical standards, including the Assisted Decision-Making (Capacity) Act 2015.
4. Responsibilities
- Manager/Person in Charge (PIC): Ensures policy implementation, staff training, and coordination with palliative care teams.
- Care Staff: Deliver compassionate care and promptly report changes in condition.
- Clinical Staff: Develop and review end-of-life care plans, administer pain relief, and liaise with palliative teams.
- All Staff: Respect confidentiality and support family members and other residents during and after the end-of-life process.
5. Advance Care Planning
- Encourage residents (and families, where appropriate) to discuss preferences for end-of-life care early in the care process.
- Record wishes regarding resuscitation (DNAR), place of death, spiritual or cultural practices, and pain management preferences.
- Review plans regularly or when health conditions change.
6. Palliative and Comfort Care
- Prioritise pain and symptom management in accordance with clinical guidelines.
- Offer support from multidisciplinary teams, including palliative care specialists, as needed.
- Facilitate spiritual or religious rites in accordance with the resident’s beliefs.
7. Family Involvement
- Include families in care planning where appropriate.
- Provide emotional support and practical information throughout the dying process.
- Allow flexible visiting and overnight stays when a resident is nearing death.
8. Environment and Dignity
- Ensure a peaceful, private, and respectful environment.
- Support cultural, religious, and personal rituals before and after death.
- Handle the deceased with dignity and ensure families are given time to say goodbye.
9. After-Death Protocol
- Notify relevant authorities and professionals promptly.
- Respect the resident’s and family’s wishes regarding funeral arrangements.
- Offer bereavement support to families and staff.
- Record and report death in accordance with HIQA and legal requirements.
10. Staff Training
All staff will receive training in end-of-life care, including communication skills, symptom management, and emotional support.
11. Monitoring and Review
- Audits will be conducted annually or following any significant incident.
- Feedback from families and staff will be reviewed to improve care standards.
- This policy will be reviewed annually or sooner if required by changes in legislation or best practice.
Secure Living Support Services Ltd
Risk Management Policy
Policy No.: SLSS-P016
Effective Date: [Insert Date]
Review Date: [Insert Date]
1. Purpose
The purpose of this policy is to establish a structured and proactive approach to identifying, assessing, managing, and monitoring risks across all areas of operation within Secure Living Support Services Ltd. The goal is to protect service users, staff, visitors, property, reputation, and business continuity while ensuring legal and regulatory compliance.
2. Scope
This policy applies to all employees, agency staff, contractors, volunteers, and management personnel operating under Secure Living Support Services Ltd. It encompasses all service areas including residential care, administration, financial operations, health and safety, and regulatory compliance.
3. Definitions
- Risk: The chance of something happening that will have an impact on the achievement of objectives.
- Risk Assessment: The process of evaluating and prioritising risks to determine how they should be managed.
- Risk Register: A central record of all identified risks, their impact, likelihood, controls, and mitigation actions.
4. Principles
- Promote a risk-aware culture across all levels of the organisation.
- Identify, assess, and manage risks in a timely and systematic manner.
- Comply with HIQA regulations, the Health Act 2007, and the National Standards for Residential Services.
- Ensure accountability for risk ownership and control implementation.
- Continuously review and improve risk management practices.
5. Responsibilities
- Board of Directors/Management: Oversee and support the implementation of the risk management framework.
- Person in Charge (PIC): Ensure that operational risks are identified, documented, and managed effectively.
- Staff: Report potential risks or hazards immediately and follow agreed control measures.
- Health & Safety Representative: Monitor physical and environmental risks and ensure controls are in place.
6. Risk Identification
Risks may arise in various forms including:
- Operational Risks: Staffing shortages, medication errors, equipment failure
- Health & Safety Risks: Fire hazards, manual handling injuries, infection control breaches
- Financial Risks: Funding instability, fraud, unapproved spending
- Reputational Risks: Media exposure, service complaints, regulatory breaches
- Strategic Risks: Service model changes, partnership failures, planning restrictions
All risks must be reported and logged using the Risk Assessment Form and added to the central Risk Register.
7. Risk Assessment and Evaluation
Risks will be assessed using a Risk Matrix that evaluates:
- Likelihood (1–5): The chance of the risk occurring
- Impact (1–5): The severity of consequences if it occurs
- Risk Rating: Likelihood × Impact = Total Risk Score
Scores will be categorised as:
- Low (1–5) – Acceptable, monitor
- Medium (6–12) – Requires action and review
- High (13–25) – Immediate action required
8. Control Measures
Control measures may include:
- Policies and procedures
- Staff training and supervision
- Equipment maintenance and inspections
- Emergency response planning
- Regular audits and reviews
The effectiveness of controls will be reviewed regularly.
9. Monitoring and Review
- The Risk Register will be reviewed monthly by management.
- All incidents and near misses will be analysed for trends.
- Policy will be reviewed annually or following any serious incident, legal change, or service expansion.
- An external audit may be conducted annually to validate risk management practices.
10. Training
All staff will receive training on:
- The importance of risk management
- How to identify and report risks
- Use of risk assessment tools and forms
- Emergency response procedures
