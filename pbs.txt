Positive Behaviour Support and Restrictive Practice Policy
Section 1: Purpose
1. Purpose
The purpose of this policy is to promote a culture of positive behaviour support (PBS) and ensure
that any use of restrictive practice is justified, proportionate, and in line with human rights principles.
This policy aims to:
- Ensure that all staff promote dignity, independence, and respectful interactions
- Prevent and reduce behaviours of concern through proactive, person-centred approaches
- Ensure restrictive practices are only used as a last resort, in line with national guidance and HIQA
standards
- Comply with the Health Act 2007, HIQA's Positive Behaviour Support Guidance (2023), and all
relevant legislation and ethical standards

Section 3: Principles
3. Principles
This policy is guided by the following principles, in line with HIQA's Positive Behaviour Support
Guidance (2023) and the UN Convention on the Rights of Persons with Disabilities (UNCRPD):
3.1 Person-Centred Approach
- Each resident's unique strengths, preferences, needs, and communication style must inform their
support plan.
- Positive behaviour support must be tailored to the individual, not a one-size-fits-all model.
3.2 Least Restrictive Option
- All staff must use the least restrictive intervention possible to ensure safety and dignity.
- Restrictive practices are only used as a last resort, when all other methods have failed and there is
an immediate risk.
3.3 Human Rights and Consent
- Residents have the right to freedom, dignity, bodily autonomy, and informed decision-making.
- Any use of restrictive practice must be:
  - Legally compliant
  - Time-limited
  - Documented and reviewed
3.4 Prevention First
- Focus is placed on understanding the causes of behaviours of concern and implementing
proactive supports to prevent escalation.
- Behaviour support plans should include triggers, communication cues, and de-escalation
techniques.
3.5 Transparency and Accountability
- All practices are subject to governance, oversight, and audit.
- Families, advocates, and external regulators are encouraged to participate in review processes.
- Use of any restrictive practice must be reported, recorded, and monitored.

Section 4: Positive Behaviour Support (PBS)
4. Positive Behaviour Support (PBS)
Positive Behaviour Support (PBS) is a proactive, person-centred approach used to understand and
respond to behaviours of concern in a way that enhances the individual's quality of life and respects
their rights.
4.1 Goals of PBS
- Promote independence, dignity, and participation
- Reduce the frequency and severity of behaviours of concern
- Improve communication and emotional regulation
- Prevent the need for restrictive interventions
4.2 Key Components
PBS involves:
- Functional Behaviour Assessment (FBA): Understanding the cause and purpose of behaviours
- Individual Behaviour Support Plans (BSPs): Tailored plans that include:
  - Triggers and early warning signs
  - Positive strategies and preventative approaches
  - De-escalation and calming techniques
  - Staff responsibilities and support roles
- Skill-building: Helping residents develop alternative behaviours that meet their needs
- Environment adaptations: Making changes to reduce distress or overstimulation
- Staff training and consistency: Ensuring all staff respond in a consistent, respectful manner
4.3 Inclusion and Collaboration
- PBS planning involves the resident, their family/representatives, keyworkers, clinicians, and
external specialists where needed
- Consent and participation from the resident are sought at every stage

Section 5: Restrictive Practices
5. Restrictive Practices
Restrictive practices refer to any intervention or action that limits an individual's rights, movement,
liberty, or decision-making, typically used to prevent harm. These practices must always be:
- Proportionate
- Time-limited
- Legally compliant
- Used only as a last resort
5.1 Categories of Restrictive Practices
HIQA identifies the following types of restrictive practice:
- Physical Restraint: Use of bodily force to restrict movement (e.g. holding arms during escalation).
- Mechanical Restraint: Use of devices or equipment that restrict free movement (e.g. locked lap
belts, mittens).
- Environmental Restraint: Restricting access to areas, items, or exits (e.g. locked doors, removing
phones).
- Chemical Restraint: Use of medication primarily to manage behaviour, not for treatment of a
diagnosed condition.
- Seclusion: Isolating a resident in a room or space where they cannot leave freely.
- Psychological/Relational Restraint: Using intimidation, threats, or power dynamics to coerce or
control.
5.2 Legal and Ethical Obligations
- Restrictive practices must comply with the Health Act 2007, Mental Health Act 2001, and HIQA
Standards.
- They must be clearly recorded, regularly reviewed, and authorised where required.
- Consent should be sought whenever possible. Use without consent must be justified and
documented.
5.3 Governance and Oversight
- Every instance of restrictive practice must be:
  - Logged in the Restrictive Practice Register
  - Reviewed by the Person in Charge (PIC)
  - Reported to HIQA, where required
- The organisation commits to reducing the use of restrictive practices over time.

Section 6: Governance
6. Governance
Governance ensures that all restrictive practices are monitored, reviewed, and reduced through
robust oversight and accountability structures.
6.1 Roles and Responsibilities
- Person in Charge (PIC):
  - Ensures all incidents involving restrictive practices are reported, reviewed, and documented
  - Signs off on all restrictive interventions used
  - Leads the review of behaviour support plans and audits restrictive practice logs
- Senior Management Team:
  - Reviews all restrictive practice data monthly to identify trends
  - Ensures resources are allocated for staff training and PBS implementation
  - Approves and monitors continuous improvement plans
- Behaviour Support Specialist/Clinician (if applicable):
  - Develops and updates Behaviour Support Plans (BSPs)
  - Provides training and coaching to staff on de-escalation and positive approaches
  - Participates in post-incident reviews and strategy updates
6.2 Restrictive Practice Register
A secure, central Restrictive Practice Register is maintained, recording:
- Type of restriction
- Date, time, and duration
- Reason for use
- Staff involved
- Resident's response
- Review outcome and actions taken
The register is reviewed monthly by the PIC and quarterly by senior management.
6.3 Internal Review
- A Restrictive Practice Oversight Group (or equivalent) meets quarterly to analyse:
  - Frequency and patterns
  - Outcomes and alternatives
  - Any breaches or concerns
- Actions are taken to address inappropriate or repeated use.

Section 7: Staff Training
7. Staff Training
Staff training is critical to ensuring that Positive Behaviour Support (PBS) is implemented effectively
and that the use of restrictive practices is minimised, ethical, and legally compliant.
7.1 Mandatory Training
All staff must complete training in the following areas:
- Positive Behaviour Support (PBS)
- De-escalation and Crisis Intervention
- Understanding Restrictive Practices
- Safeguarding Vulnerable Adults
- Human Rights and Consent
- HIQA's National Standards and PBS Guidance
7.2 Refresher Training
- PBS and restrictive practice training must be refreshed every 2 years or more frequently as
required by policy or regulatory changes.
- Records of all training sessions are maintained in each staff member's training register.
7.3 Competency Assessment
- Staff understanding of PBS and safe use of interventions is regularly assessed through
supervision, observation, and post-incident reviews.
- Where staff competency is lacking, additional coaching or retraining is arranged immediately.
7.4 Specialist Support
- Behaviour Specialists or external professionals may provide enhanced training and workshops for
complex behavioural needs.
- All training must be aligned with the Health Act 2007 and best practice guidelines.

Section 8: Oversight
8. Oversight
Secure Living Support Services Ltd is committed to maintaining a culture of transparency, learning,
and continuous improvement in relation to restrictive practices. Oversight mechanisms ensure that
use of such practices is lawful, ethical, monitored, and consistently reduced.
8.1 Restrictive Practice Oversight Group
- A multidisciplinary group meets quarterly to review:
  - Trends and frequency of restrictive practice
  - Compliance with internal policy and HIQA guidelines
  - Effectiveness of behaviour support plans
  - Opportunities for reduction and staff retraining
- The group may include: PIC, senior manager, external behaviour specialist, clinical advisor, and
service user representatives (where appropriate).
8.2 Quarterly Reporting
- A written report is produced every 3 months detailing:
  - Number and type of restrictive interventions used
  - Residents involved and associated outcomes
  - Incidents where de-escalation strategies succeeded or failed
  - Actions taken to reduce future use
- These reports are reviewed by the Registered Provider and used to update organisational risk and
quality plans.
8.3 HIQA Notification and Audit
- All notifiable restrictive practices are submitted via the appropriate HIQA portal.
- Internal audits are conducted twice yearly to ensure:
  - Logs are complete and accurate
  - Justifications are clearly recorded
  - Debriefs and reviews have been completed
  - Staff are acting in line with best practice

Section 9: Consent
9. Consent
Consent is a core principle in the use of Positive Behaviour Support (PBS) and any restrictive
practices. All interventions must respect the individual's legal rights, autonomy, and capacity to
make informed choices.
9.1 Informed Consent
- Residents must be provided with information in a format they can understand (e.g. easy-read
documents, visuals).
- Staff must explain:
  - The nature and purpose of the support
  - Any potential risks or restrictions
  - The resident's right to refuse
- Consent must be freely given, specific, and recorded in the care/support plan.
9.2 Capacity and Supported Decision-Making
- If a resident is deemed to lack decision-making capacity:
  - The Assisted Decision-Making (Capacity) Act 2015 applies
  - Staff must involve a Decision-Making Representative, family, or advocate
  - All decisions must be in the best interest of the resident
- The use of restrictive practices in such cases must be legally justified and documented
9.3 Withdrawal of Consent
- Consent can be withdrawn at any time.
- If a resident withdraws consent to a restrictive measure, it must be:
  - Respected immediately where safe to do so
  - Reviewed by the Person in Charge (PIC)
  - Documented in the Restrictive Practice Register
